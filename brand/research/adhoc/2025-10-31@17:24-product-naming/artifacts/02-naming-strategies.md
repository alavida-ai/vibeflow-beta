## Strategy 1: Spanish-Inspired Architecture

### Strategic Rationale

**Why this approach fits the brand:**
- Honors Alavida's Spanish-American cultural roots authentically (founders' identity, not appropriation)
- Creates immediate cohesion with parent brand "Alavida" ("to life" in Spanish)
- Differentiates from 100% English-language competitor naming patterns
- Sounds sophisticated and premium to English speakers (like Los Angeles, Sacramento, Florida)
- Works seamlessly across both English and Spanish-speaking markets (international scalability)
- Spanish language offers rich architectural/building vocabulary aligned with "Marketing Architecture" category

**Brand alignment:**
- **Cultural authenticity:** Spanish roots are genuine to founders, not tokenistic
- **Premium positioning:** Spanish names feel sophisticated to target audience (marketing strategists age 30-45)
- **Technical credibility:** Romance language origins signal intellectual depth over consumer-friendly simplicity
- **Category ownership:** Spanish architectural terms occupy unclaimed territory in AI marketing space

---

### Naming Principles

**What makes a good name in this category:**

1. **Authentic Spanish vocabulary** (not Spanglish or fabricated hybrids)
   - Use real Spanish words with architectural/building/systems meaning
   - Avoid Spanish words unrelated to marketing/AI/architecture
   - Must translate clearly to Spanish speakers without cultural insensitivity

2. **Conveys marketing/AI/systems meaning** (directly or metaphorically)
   - Should relate to building, designing, creating, structuring
   - Can metaphorically suggest strategy, infrastructure, foundations
   - Acceptable: abstract evocation if word has architectural etymology

3. **International pronunciation** (works in both English and Spanish)
   - Avoid difficult Spanish sounds for English speakers (rolling r's, ñ, strong j)
   - Phonetically accessible to English-speaking target market
   - Maintains Spanish authenticity while being pronounceable

4. **Premium and sophisticated** (not consumer-friendly or playful)
   - Should feel intellectual, technical, substantial
   - Avoid diminutives (-ito, -ita) that feel cute
   - Gravitas over accessibility

5. **Two-to-three syllables preferred** (memorable but substantial)
   - Not too short (feels incomplete)
   - Not too long (loses memorability)
   - Sweet spot: substantial but sayable

---

### Example Patterns to Explore

**Spanish architectural/building vocabulary:**
- **Estructura** (structure) - too literal, but shows direction
- **Cimiento** (foundation) - aligns with infrastructure ownership
- **Diseño** (design) - architect identity
- **Obra** (work/construction) - building connotation
- **Trama** (framework/structure) - systems thinking
- **Forja** (forge/foundry) - creation metaphor

**Spanish strategy/planning vocabulary:**
- **Estrategia** (strategy) - too obvious
- **Plan** (plan) - too simple
- **Esquema** (scheme/framework) - structural thinking
- **Pauta** (guideline/pattern) - systems design

**Spanish place-based vocabulary (following Alavida pattern):**
- Look for Spanish words that could sound like place names
- Consider real Spanish colonial place names related to building/founding
- Explore invented place-like combinations with architectural roots

**Compound possibilities:**
- Combine Spanish architectural term + marketing/AI concept
- Use Spanish prefix/suffix patterns (-ería, -aje, -ción)
- Consider verb forms that suggest building/creating

---

### Differentiation from "vibeflow" and Competitors

**How this differs from current naming:**

**vs. vibeflow:**
- Spanish vs. English (immediate visual/auditory differentiation)
- Architectural gravitas vs. ethereal flow (substance vs. vibe)
- Cultural specificity vs. generic tech naming
- Premium international feel vs. consumer SaaS pattern

**vs. AI marketing competitors:**
- 0% of competitors use Spanish or non-English names (blue ocean territory)
- Most competitors use English portmanteaus (Jasper, Copy.ai, Anyword)
- Spanish architectural terms occupy unclaimed semantic space
- Cultural authenticity vs. generic tech naming conventions

**Strategic advantage:**
- Defensible cultural authenticity (competitors can't claim Spanish roots without appropriation)
- Immediate brand family cohesion with Alavida
- Stands out in English-dominated AI marketing category
- Premium international positioning (works globally, not just US market)

---

### Specific Guidance for Name Generation Agents

**DO:**
- ✅ Use authentic Spanish dictionary words with architectural/building meaning
- ✅ Verify Spanish words work in both Spain and Latin America (avoid regional slang)
- ✅ Test pronunciation by English speakers (say it out loud—is it accessible?)
- ✅ Ensure word relates to marketing/AI/systems (direct or metaphorical)
- ✅ Check that word doesn't have negative connotations in Spanish or English
- ✅ Favor nouns over adjectives (more substantial)
- ✅ Consider Spanish words that sound like place names (Alavida pattern)

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Create fake Spanish words or Spanglish hybrids (inauthentic)
- ❌ Use Spanish words unrelated to architecture/marketing/AI
- ❌ Choose Spanish words that are hard for English speakers to pronounce
- ❌ Use diminutive forms (-ito, -ita) that feel cute/consumer-friendly
- ❌ Appropriate Indigenous or regional Spanish without cultural validation
- ❌ Use Spanish words that translate to "easy," "simple," "fast," "automated" (violates brand constraints)
- ❌ Choose overly literal translations of English tech terms

**Evaluation checklist:**
1. Is this a real Spanish word (not invented)?
2. Does it relate to architecture/marketing/AI/systems?
3. Can English speakers pronounce it reasonably?
4. Does it sound premium and sophisticated (not consumer-friendly)?
5. Does it honor Spanish-American culture authentically?
6. Does it work in both Spanish and English markets?
7. Is the domain likely available (.com or .ai)?

**Example thought process:**
- **Candidate:** "Fundamento" (foundation in Spanish)
- **Evaluation:** Real word ✅, architectural meaning ✅, pronounceable ✅, premium ✅, authentic ✅, international ✅
- **Domain check:** fundamento.com / fundamento.ai
- **Final assessment:** Strong candidate if domain available

## Strategy 2: Architecture/Infrastructure Metaphors

### Strategic Rationale

**Why this approach fits the brand:**
- Directly reinforces "Marketing Architecture" category ownership (0% competitor usage of architecture terminology)
- Leverages existing white space—no AI marketing competitors use architect/architecture/infrastructure language
- Aligns with core identity transformation: "Overwhelmed Tactician" → "Marketing Architect"
- Signals ownership and permanence (infrastructure you own, not SaaS you rent)
- Appeals to target audience's aspirational identity (systems builders, not button pushers)
- Differentiates from 85% of competitors who position on ease/convenience

**Brand alignment:**
- **Category creation:** "Marketing Architecture" is completely unclaimed—name reinforces category
- **Competitive moat:** Infrastructure ownership is defensible differentiation (100% of competitors use subscription models)
- **Audience resonance:** Marketing strategists use language like "foundational work," "systems thinking," "infrastructure"
- **Brand Pillar 3:** "Architect Systems, Don't Rent Automation" directly supported

---

### Naming Principles

**What makes a good name in this category:**

1. **Evokes building, structure, or systems** (not tools or utilities)
   - Should suggest construction, design, engineering
   - Metaphors from architecture, civil engineering, urban planning
   - Acceptable: abstract architectural concepts (e.g., blueprint, framework, scaffold)

2. **Signals permanence and ownership** (not services or platforms)
   - Should feel foundational, immovable, owned
   - Avoid SaaS/platform/service language patterns
   - Suggests infrastructure you control, not software you subscribe to

3. **Premium and sophisticated** (not generic tech buzzwords)
   - Avoid overused terms: "builder," "stack," "forge," "hub," "suite"
   - Favor less obvious architectural concepts with depth
   - Should feel intellectually interesting, not commodity

4. **Not literal "architect" or "architecture"** (too on-the-nose)
   - Evoke the concept without using the exact words
   - Metaphorical richness over direct labeling
   - Save "Marketing Architecture" for category, not product name

5. **Works as a standalone noun** (not modifier dependent)
   - Should work without "for marketing" qualifier
   - Strong enough to stand alone
   - Can support "by Alavida" tagline

---

### Example Patterns to Explore

**Architectural element metaphors:**
- **Foundation/basement concepts** - bedrock, groundwork, base
- **Structural systems** - framework, scaffold, truss, lattice
- **Building materials** - cornerstone, keystone, lintel
- **Design artifacts** - blueprint, schema, draft, plan
- **Construction processes** - assembly, fabrication, erection

**Infrastructure/systems metaphors:**
- **Urban planning** - grid, network, nexus, hub (avoid "hub"—overused)
- **Civil engineering** - conduit, aqueduct, viaduct
- **Mechanical systems** - mechanism, apparatus, engine (careful—can feel like automation)
- **Organizational structures** - matrix, hierarchy, lattice

**Abstract architectural concepts:**
- **Design principles** - symmetry, proportion, cantilever, tension
- **Spatial concepts** - atrium, arcade, plaza (careful—sounds like place names)
- **Architectural movements** - modernist terms, brutalist concepts (intellectual depth)

**Less obvious options:**
- **Geological/foundational** - stratum, bedrock, substrate
- **Botanical/organic architecture** - trellis, lattice (natural structure)
- **Mathematical/geometric** - tesselation, recursion (systems thinking)

---

### Differentiation from "vibeflow" and Competitors

**How this differs from current naming:**

**vs. vibeflow:**
- Structural vs. fluid (architecture vs. flow)
- Permanence vs. movement (foundation vs. vibe)
- Ownership vs. process (infrastructure vs. workflow)
- Substantive vs. ethereal (concrete vs. abstract)

**vs. AI marketing competitors:**
- 0% use architecture/infrastructure terminology (blue ocean)
- Most use generic tech terms (Writer, Jasper, Copy.ai) or automation language (Zapier-style)
- Architecture metaphors signal ownership over subscription (none claim this)
- Infrastructure suggests building capability, not renting convenience

**Strategic advantage:**
- Unclaimed semantic territory in AI marketing category
- Reinforces "Marketing Architecture" category creation
- Defensible against competitors (can't credibly claim architecture without changing business model)
- Aligns with audience's aspirational identity (architect, not user)

---

### Specific Guidance for Name Generation Agents

**DO:**
- ✅ Use architectural/infrastructure metaphors that signal building and systems
- ✅ Favor less obvious terms (not "builder," "stack," "platform")
- ✅ Test that name suggests ownership/permanence, not service/utility
- ✅ Ensure name works standalone (doesn't need "for marketing" qualifier)
- ✅ Check domain availability (.com or .ai)
- ✅ Verify name isn't overused in tech/SaaS (Google it)
- ✅ Consider names that could work across product line (scalable)

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Use literal "architect" or "architecture" (save for category)
- ❌ Choose generic tech buzzwords (builder, stack, forge, hub, suite)
- ❌ Pick names that feel like tools or utilities (wrench, hammer, lever)
- ❌ Use automation-focused metaphors (engine, automation, machine)
- ❌ Select names that suggest ease/convenience (shortcut, express, rapid)
- ❌ Go too abstract (name should still suggest building/systems)
- ❌ Use names already claimed by major dev tools (avoid GitHub pattern territory)

**Evaluation checklist:**
1. Does this evoke architecture, building, or infrastructure?
2. Does it signal ownership/permanence (not service/rental)?
3. Is it premium and sophisticated (not generic tech buzzword)?
4. Does it differentiate from "vibeflow" clearly?
5. Is it unclaimed by AI marketing competitors?
6. Can it stand alone without "for marketing" qualifier?
7. Is the domain available?

**Example thought process:**
- **Candidate:** "Keystone" (central architectural support)
- **Evaluation:** Architectural ✅, permanence ✅, premium ✅, differentiated ✅, likely unclaimed in AI marketing ✅
- **Domain check:** keystone.com / keystone.ai
- **Concern:** May be used in other industries (research required)
- **Final assessment:** Strong if domain available and not crowded

## Strategy 3: Marketing + AI Semantic Blends

### Strategic Rationale

**Why this approach fits the brand:**
- Explicitly signals dual nature of product (marketing intelligence + AI capability)
- Creates memorable portmanteaus that own specific semantic territory
- Allows for invented words that are trademarkable and domain-available
- Differentiates from single-concept names (pure AI or pure marketing)
- Technical sophistication through compound construction (not consumer-friendly)
- Can encode category positioning directly in the name itself

**Brand alignment:**
- **Category clarity:** Name itself communicates "AI for marketing" without explanation
- **Technical credibility:** Portmanteaus/compounds signal sophistication to tech-literate audience
- **Competitive differentiation:** Avoids patterns like Jasper, Copy.ai, Writer (different construction logic)
- **Brand Pillar 4:** "Enhance Humans, Don't Replace Them"—blend suggests collaboration, not replacement

---

### Naming Principles

**What makes a good name in this category:**

1. **Fuses marketing and AI concepts authentically** (not forced)
   - Both components should contribute clear meaning
   - Fusion should feel natural, not contrived
   - Pronunciation should be smooth (no awkward consonant clusters)

2. **Technical sophistication** (not consumer-friendly or playful)
   - Should feel like a technical term, not a brand mascot
   - Gravitas over cuteness
   - Can be abstract/invented but must feel substantial

3. **Differentiates from competitor patterns** (avoid Copy.ai, Jasper.ai logic)
   - Not "[Marketing Term].ai"
   - Not single-word AI repurposing (Jasper, Writer)
   - Not playful animal/character names (Hootsuite pattern)

4. **Memorable and pronounceable** (despite being compound)
   - Should roll off tongue despite complexity
   - Avoid tongue-twisters or ambiguous pronunciation
   - Test: can someone spell it after hearing it once?

5. **Suggests collaboration, not replacement** (augmentation over automation)
   - Blend should suggest partnership between marketing + AI
   - Avoid terms that imply AI works alone
   - Should feel like "marketing enhanced by AI," not "AI does marketing"

---

### Example Patterns to Explore

**Portmanteau structures (word A + word B):**
- **Market** + **Intelligence** = Marketelligence
- **Strategy** + **Synthesis** = Stratesys
- **Brand** + **Architect** = Branditect
- **Content** + **Intelligence** = Contentelligence
- **Campaign** + **Infrastructure** = Campinfra

**Compound word structures (word + word):**
- **Marketing** + **Nexus** = MarketingNexus (but feels long)
- **Brand** + **Schema** = BrandSchema
- **Strategy** + **Graph** = StrategyGraph
- **Content** + **Matrix** = ContentMatrix

**Prefix/suffix blends:**
- **Auto-** (self) + marketing term = Autostrategist (careful—"auto" suggests automation)
- **Meta-** (beyond) + marketing term = Metacampaign
- **Syn-** (together) + marketing term = Synbrand, Syncontent
- Marketing term + **-ify** = Brandify (careful—overused SaaS pattern)
- Marketing term + **-ics** = Brandics, Marketrics

**Latin/Greek root blends:**
- **Vox** (voice) + marketing = Voxmarket
- **Lex** (language) + brand = Lexbrand
- **Logos** (word/reason) + content = Logocontent
- **Syn** (together) + strategy = Synstrategy

---

### Differentiation from "vibeflow" and Competitors

**How this differs from current naming:**

**vs. vibeflow:**
- Explicit dual-concept vs. single metaphor (marketing+AI vs. flow)
- Technical compound vs. organic metaphor (engineered vs. natural)
- Direct category signal vs. abstract evocation (clarity vs. vibe)
- Invented fusion vs. existing words (proprietary vs. common)

**vs. AI marketing competitors:**
- Most use single concept + ".ai" (Copy.ai, Writer.ai, Wordtune.ai)
- Or repurposed single words (Jasper, Rytr, Writesonic)
- Blends allow dual-concept encoding (marketing + AI in one term)
- Avoids overused patterns (not "[word].ai" or single mystical name)

**Strategic advantage:**
- Portmanteau/compound is ownable and trademarkable (invented term)
- Immediately communicates dual nature (marketing + AI)
- Domain availability higher for invented fusions
- Differentiates from both consumer SaaS and AI tool naming patterns

---

### Specific Guidance for Name Generation Agents

**DO:**
- ✅ Fuse marketing and AI concepts that both contribute meaning
- ✅ Test pronunciation (say it out loud 5 times—does it flow?)
- ✅ Ensure blend feels technical/sophisticated, not cute/consumer-friendly
- ✅ Verify domain availability for invented compounds
- ✅ Check that name doesn't accidentally spell something unfortunate
- ✅ Favor 3-4 syllable compounds (substantial but memorable)
- ✅ Consider Latin/Greek roots for intellectual depth

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Use "[marketing term].ai" pattern (overdone, feels like domain hack)
- ❌ Force awkward combinations that don't pronounce smoothly
- ❌ Blend words that both mean the same thing (redundant)
- ❌ Create playful/cute portmanteaus (violates premium positioning)
- ❌ Use "auto-" prefix (suggests automation/replacement)
- ❌ Mimic existing AI tool patterns (Jasper, Writer, Copy.ai logic)
- ❌ Blend so aggressively that meaning is lost (must still parse)

**Evaluation checklist:**
1. Does it fuse marketing and AI concepts clearly?
2. Is it pronounceable and relatively easy to spell?
3. Does it feel technical/sophisticated (not consumer-friendly)?
4. Does it differentiate from competitor patterns?
5. Does it suggest collaboration/augmentation (not replacement)?
6. Is the compound memorable despite being invented?
7. Is the domain available?

**Example thought process:**
- **Candidate:** "Branditect" (Brand + Architect)
- **Evaluation:** Dual concept ✅, pronounceable ✅, technical ✅, differentiated ✅, collaboration implied ✅
- **Domain check:** branditect.com / branditect.ai
- **Concern:** Very close to literal "architect" (may be too on-the-nose)
- **Final assessment:** Strong if domain available, but consider if too literal

## Strategy 4: Place-Based Naming (Following Alavida Pattern)

### Strategic Rationale

**Why this approach fits the brand:**
- Mirrors Alavida naming logic (sounds like southern USA/Spanish colonial place name)
- Creates immediate brand family cohesion through sonic similarity
- Place names convey permanence, ownership, foundation (you "own land," not "rent tools")
- Spanish colonial place names signal premium/sophisticated (Los Angeles, San Francisco, Sacramento)
- Allows for both real and invented place names with architectural/marketing etymology
- Differentiated from generic tech naming (evocative vs. descriptive)

**Brand alignment:**
- **Cultural authenticity:** Spanish-American founders, Spanish colonial heritage
- **Permanence/ownership:** Places are permanent and ownable (aligns with infrastructure positioning)
- **Premium positioning:** Spanish colonial place names feel sophisticated to English speakers
- **Brand Pillar 2:** "Own Your Stack, Own Your Destiny"—place names suggest ownership
- **Alavida cohesion:** Immediate sonic/stylistic brand family recognition

---

### Naming Principles

**What makes a good name in this category:**

1. **Sounds like a southern USA or Spanish colonial place** (real or invented)
   - Phonetic patterns: Los/Las/San/Santa + noun, or Spanish geographic terms
   - Should evoke California, Florida, Texas, New Mexico place names
   - Can be invented but must sound plausibly real

2. **Subtle relation to marketing/AI/systems** (etymological or metaphorical)
   - Place name's etymology should connect to architecture/building/marketing
   - Can be abstract/poetic rather than literal
   - Acceptable: tenuous connection if name feels right

3. **Premium and distinctive** (not generic or common)
   - Should sound more like "San Francisco" than "Springfield"
   - Sophisticated over folksy
   - Memorable and ownable

4. **Works as a standalone noun** (not modifier-dependent)
   - Should function like a place name: "We use [Name]"
   - Can support "by Alavida" tagline
   - Doesn't need "Marketing Architecture" qualifier to make sense

5. **Two-to-four syllables** (memorable length)
   - Long enough for substance
   - Short enough for recall
   - Sweet spot: 3 syllables (like "Alavida," "Los Angeles")

---

### Example Patterns to Explore

**Real Spanish colonial place name patterns:**
- **Los/Las + [noun]** - Los Angeles (the angels), Las Vegas (the meadows)
- **San/Santa + [saint]** - San Francisco, Santa Fe, San Diego
- **[Spanish geographic term]** - Sierra (mountains), Mesa (plateau), Costa (coast)
- **[Spanish architectural term]** - Plaza, Pueblo, Alameda (tree-lined avenue)

**Invented place-like Spanish names:**
- Spanish architectural/building terms that sound like places
- **Fundación** (foundation) - sounds like a place
- **Estructura** (structure) - could be a place name
- **Diseño** (design) - sounds place-like with right context
- **Trama** (framework/structure) - could work

**Marketing/AI-related invented place names:**
- Names that etymologically relate to marketing/strategy/AI but sound like places
- Example: "Strategia" (invented, sounds like a Spanish colonial city)
- Example: "Mercado" (market in Spanish, sounds like a place—though literal)

**Hybrid invented names:**
- Combine Spanish architectural + geographic to create fictional place
- Example: "Sierra Diseño" (design mountains) - feels like a place
- Example: "Costa Marca" (brand coast) - invented but place-like

---

### Differentiation from "vibeflow" and Competitors

**How this differs from current naming:**

**vs. vibeflow:**
- Place name vs. process/vibe (location vs. flow)
- Spanish/colonial vs. English/modern (cultural vs. universal)
- Permanence vs. movement (fixed geography vs. flowing energy)
- Sophistication vs. ethereal (grounded vs. abstract)

### Specific Guidance for Name Generation Agents

**DO:**

## Strategy 5: Abstract/Evocative Approach

### Strategic Rationale

**Why this approach fits the brand:**
- Premium positioning through intellectual sophistication (not immediately obvious)
- Sharp differentiation from generic AI tool names (Copy.ai, Writer, Jasper patterns)
- Allows for invented words that are ownable, trademarkable, domain-available
- Complexity acceptable—audience is sophisticated and sees learning as competitive advantage
- Can evoke quality/capability/systems without literal meaning
- Opens territory beyond marketing/AI semantic constraints (conceptual freedom)

**Brand alignment:**
- **Premium positioning:** Abstract/evocative names feel high-end (like luxury brands)
- **Complexity as moat:** Target audience doesn't need instant clarity—sophistication is aspirational
- **Brand Pillar 1:** "Capability Over Convenience"—name doesn't apologize for complexity
- **Intellectual provocation:** Abstract names invite interpretation (aligns with voice attribute)
- **Differentiation:** Avoids crowded semantic space of marketing/AI terms

---

### Naming Principles

**What makes a good name in this category:**

1. **No literal meaning but evokes quality/capability/systems** (conceptual resonance)
   - Should feel substantial and meaningful without being descriptive
   - Evocation over denotation
   - Can suggest technical sophistication, precision, architecture without stating it

2. **Premium and distinctive** (not generic or commodity)
   - Should sound expensive, rare, sophisticated
   - Luxury brand feel over consumer SaaS
   - Memorable through uniqueness, not clarity

3. **Sharp differentiation from generic AI tool names** (avoid overused patterns)
   - Not single-word AI repurposing (Jasper, Writer)
   - Not "[word].ai" domain hack (Copy.ai, Wordtune.ai)
   - Not playful/cute consumer SaaS (Zapier, Asana, Hootsuite)

4. **Invented words or obscure references** (ownable and trademarkable)
   - Can be completely invented (neologisms)
   - Can be obscure Latin/Greek/Spanish terms with depth
   - Should feel intentional, not random

5. **Phonetically sophisticated** (rolls off tongue with gravitas)
   - Pronunciation should feel weighty, not light
   - Acceptable complexity—audience is technical
   - Should sound premium when spoken aloud

---

### Example Patterns to Explore

**Invented neologisms (completely new words):**
- Create words that sound technical/sophisticated but are invented
- Use Latin/Greek/Romance language phonetic patterns for credibility
- Example logic: "Nexum" (sounds Latin, evokes connection/systems)
- Example logic: "Stratix" (strategy + suffix, technical feel)
- Example logic: "Arcova" (arc + suffix, architectural suggestion)

**Obscure Latin/Greek terms:**
- **Tectonic roots:** "Tecton" (builder in Greek), "Structura" (structure in Latin)
- **Design/order concepts:** "Kosmos" (order/universe), "Taxis" (arrangement)
- **Creation/making:** "Poiesis" (making/creation), "Demiurge" (craftsman/creator)
- **System/framework:** "Schema" (form/shape), "Systema" (organized whole)

**Abstract quality descriptors:**
- Words that evoke precision, excellence, sophistication
- **Precision/exactness:** "Veritas" (truth), "Exacta," "Precision" derivatives
- **Excellence/mastery:** "Apex," "Summit," "Zenith" (careful—overused in tech)
- **Clarity/insight:** "Lucid," "Crystalline," derivatives

**Phonetic invention with evocative suffixes:**
- **-ix / -yx suffixes:** Stratix, Brandyx, Architectix (technical feel)
- **-um / -ium suffixes:** Nexum, Structurium, Brandium (Latin gravitas)
- **-is / -os suffixes:** Brandetis, Marketos, Strategis (Greek sophistication)
- **-ara / -era suffixes:** Brandara, Marketera (Romance language elegance)

**Conceptual metaphor names:**
- Abstract concepts related to systems/architecture/quality
- **Natural phenomena:** "Quartz" (precision/structure), "Obsidian" (sharp/premium)
- **Mathematical/geometric:** "Tensor" (multidimensional), "Vector" (direction)
- **Musical/harmonic:** "Cadence" (rhythm/structure), "Harmonic" (systems alignment)

---

### Differentiation from "vibeflow" and Competitors

**How this differs from current naming:**

**vs. vibeflow:**
- Abstract invented vs. metaphorical English (neologism vs. compound word)
- Sophistication vs. accessibility (intellectual vs. intuitive)
- Evocation vs. description (suggests vs. states)
- Premium positioning vs. flow/energy (gravitas vs. movement)

**vs. AI marketing competitors:**
- Most use literal descriptors (Copy.ai, Writer) or single repurposed words (Jasper, Rytr)
